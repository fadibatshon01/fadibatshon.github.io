# The Carry Trade Doesn't Care About Your Thesis

Uncovered interest rate parity says you can't earn a risk-free return by borrowing in a low-rate currency and lending in a high-rate one — the high-yield currency should depreciate by exactly the rate differential. Forty years of FX data say the opposite. The carry trade works most of the time, and then it spectacularly doesn't.

The standard explanation is "peso problem" — most of the time you collect the spread, occasionally you blow up. But the more interesting answer is structural. The carry trade is a positioning trade, not a value trade. As long as flows are moving toward the high-yielder, the trade pays. When flows reverse, the trade unwinds violently, because everyone is positioned the same way.

That tells you something useful: **the trade's risk profile is reflexive**. The carry trade gets safer as more capital piles in — more flow, more spread compression, more carry — right up until the moment it gets dangerous, when positioning is one-sided and any shock forces a stampede for the exit.

Equity carry — yield-on-debt strategies, dividend-yield baskets, REITs, MLPs — has the same shape. Pays steadily, drawdown comes from a regime change you didn't predict, and the unwind is correlated with everyone else's unwind.

The bottom line: carry isn't about being right on the macro thesis. It's about being early to the position and willing to exit before everyone else.
