# A Framework for Reading Central Bank Press Conferences

Most of what a central banker says in a press conference is choreography. The statement is pre-negotiated, the talking points are rehearsed, and the goal is to move the curve as little as possible while still committing to something. The signal isn't in the words — it's in the omissions, the hedges, and the pauses.

Here's the framework I use.

## 1. Read the statement before the Q&A

The prepared statement is the negotiated position. Compare it line-by-line to the previous one. Three things matter: words that changed, words that disappeared, and qualifiers that softened or hardened. Everything in the Q&A is downstream of the statement.

## 2. Track what the chair refuses to be drawn on

When a reporter asks a question the chair doesn't want to answer, watch the deflection. A central banker who says "we will be data-dependent" is telling you they don't want to commit. A central banker who says "I'm not going to speculate on that" is telling you the answer would move markets.

## 3. The pauses

A 1.5-second pause before answering a rate-path question is more informative than the answer itself. Press conferences are practiced; pauses mean the chair is choosing between scripts.

## 4. Watch the dissents

In a system with voting members, the dissent matters more than the median. A 9–2 hold with both dissents calling for cuts is a different signal than an 11–0 hold with hawkish minutes. The vote tally is the leading indicator; the statement is the lagging one.

The bottom line: don't trade the press conference. Trade what the press conference *didn't say*.
