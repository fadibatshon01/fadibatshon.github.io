# Concentration Risk Isn't Going Anywhere

The arithmetic hasn't changed in years. A small handful of names sits on most of the market-cap weight of the S&P 500, which means they sit on most of the variance, most of the upside, and most of the downside any given quarter. Owning "the market" is no longer particularly diversified — it is, to a first approximation, owning that handful.

The reflexive response is to flag this as a bubble symptom. I think that's the wrong frame. Concentration is a structural feature of index construction in an era of platform economics — winner-take-most businesses are larger, more profitable, and more enduring than the textbooks predicted in 2005.

> The interesting question isn't *whether* the index is concentrated. It's how an investor who already owns the index thinks about adding active exposure on top.

If your index allocation already gives you 30%+ exposure to the top names, adding "high-conviction" calls on those same names doubles up a position you already hold. The risk-adjusted edge of an active overlay disappears when the active and the passive aren't actually independent.

The bottom line: concentration risk isn't a bug to fix on the index side. It's a constraint to respect on the active side.
